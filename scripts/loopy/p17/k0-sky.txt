Sunlight isn’t actually white—it’s made of all the colors of the rainbow mixed together. When this light enters Earth’s atmosphere, it runs into tiny molecules and particles in the air. These air particles act like a filter, interacting with the different colors in the sunlight as they travel downward.

The key is that blue light has shorter, tighter waves compared to other colors like red or yellow. Because of this, blue light bounces around more easily when it hits air molecules, spreading out in all directions. This process is called scattering, and it means that whenever you look up, you’re seeing light that’s been scattered by the atmosphere rather than light that’s traveling straight down. That’s why the daytime sky appears blue instead of any other color.

The other colors, like red and yellow, have longer waves that slip through the atmosphere more easily without bouncing around. That’s why they don’t dominate the sky during the day. At sunrise or sunset, sunlight has to travel through much more atmosphere, which is why those longer waves get scattered instead and paint the sky in reds and oranges. But during daylight hours, the constant bouncing of blue light keeps the sky looking bright and blue.<|im_end|>
