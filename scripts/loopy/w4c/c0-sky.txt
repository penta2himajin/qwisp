The sky looks blue because of how sunlight interacts with Earth’s atmosphere. Sunlight might appear white, but it actually contains all the colors of the rainbow mixed together. When this light travels through the air, it bumps into tiny gas molecules and other particles. These collisions cause the light to scatter in different directions, with shorter colors like blue and violet bouncing around more than longer colors like red and yellow.

This scattering process is called Rayleigh scattering, and it’s the reason we see blue during the day. Because blue light has shorter waves, it gets scattered by air molecules much more easily than red or orange light, which has longer waves. As a result, blue light spreads across the sky and reaches our eyes from every direction, while the longer colors mostly keep traveling straight through the atmosphere.

Even though violet light is scattered even more than blue, the sky still looks blue to us. This happens because our eyes are naturally more sensitive to blue, and the sun itself produces less violet light than blue. Additionally, some violet light gets absorbed by the upper atmosphere, leaving blue as the dominant color we perceive. So, when you look up during daylight, you see a sky painted in shades of blue.
